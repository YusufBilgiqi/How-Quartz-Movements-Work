What Makes Quartz So Accurate?
No Gravity Effect
A quartz crystal is so tiny and light that gravity has essentially no effect on its vibration rate. A mechanical watch needs careful balancing to compensate ‚Äî quartz simply doesn't have the problem.
Stable Temperature
Quartz is far less sensitive to temperature changes than a metal pendulum or balance spring. Temperature changes that would noticeably affect a mechanical watch barely register on a quartz oscillator.
No Air Pressure Effect
Because the quartz crystal is sealed in a small container, changes in barometric pressure have no effect on its frequency. Traditional pendulum clocks are noticeably affected by air pressure changes.
Consistent High Frequency
At 32,768 vibrations per second, small errors average out over time. A mechanical watch balance oscillates at only 3‚Äì4 Hz ‚Äî every tiny disturbance has a far greater proportional effect on accuracy.

Electricity is invisible, which makes it feel mysterious. But it behaves in a very predictable way ‚Äî and comparing it to water flowing through pipes makes it click immediately.

Rule 1 ‚Äî Voltage (Pressure)
Higher voltage = more current. If you double the battery voltage, you double the current flowing through the circuit. The pump pushes harder, more water flows.
Rule 2 ‚Äî Resistance
Higher resistance = less current. A more restrictive component allows less current to pass, even at the same voltage. Narrow pipes restrict water ‚Äî resistors restrict electrons.

Chapter 3 ¬∑ The Basics

Magnetism

The stepper motor relies on one key trick: a coil of wire carrying electrical current creates a magnetic field. Change the direction of the current, change the magnetic poles ‚Äî and the rotor spins.

3.1
Key Magnetic Facts for Watchmakers
Opposite poles attract
North pulls South. This is what aligns the rotor when current flows ‚Äî the rotor's permanent magnet aligns with the magnetic poles created in the stator.
Like poles repel
North pushes North. When the current in the coil reverses, the stator poles flip. Now like poles face each other ‚Äî and repulsion spins the rotor.
Coil = electromagnet
Passing current through a coil of wire creates a magnetic field around it. Reverse the current, reverse the poles. This is the core principle of the stepper motor.
Permanent magnets
The rotor contains a permanent magnet ‚Äî it keeps its magnetism without any current flowing. The stator is only magnetised when current flows through the coil.
Chapter 4 ¬∑ The Motor

The Stepper Motor

The stepper motor converts electrical pulses into precise rotational steps. Each pulse from the circuit turns the rotor exactly 180 degrees ‚Äî and that drives the gear train forward by one step.

4.1
The Three Components
Rotor
A tiny disc containing a permanent magnet. It has a North and South pole. It's connected to the gear train via a pinion. When the rotor spins, the train moves.
Stator
A shaped piece of magnetic material surrounding the rotor. When current flows through the coil, the stator becomes magnetised and creates North/South pole faces that push or pull the rotor.
Coil
Thousands of turns of fine copper wire wound around the stator. When the circuit sends current through the coil, the stator becomes an electromagnet. Reversing the current reverses the magnetic poles.
Result
Reverse the current ‚Üí stator poles flip ‚Üí like poles repel ‚Üí rotor spins 180¬∞ ‚Üí pinion advances the gear train ‚Üí hands move forward exactly one step.

Chapter 5 ¬∑ The Motor

Direction & Current Pulses

Two problems had to be solved: making the rotor always turn the same direction, and not wasting battery power by keeping the current on continuously.

5.1
Controlling Direction ‚Äî The Offset Rest Position

Left to itself, the rotor could spin either clockwise or counter-clockwise ‚Äî both directions are equally possible. This is a problem: running the hands backwards is useless.

The solution is clever: the stator pole faces are shaped asymmetrically. The smallest gap between the rotor and stator is not at the horizontal centre ‚Äî it's a few degrees offset in the clockwise direction. This means the rotor always comes to rest slightly rotated clockwise.

When the current reverses, the repulsion effect now only has one option ‚Äî push the rotor clockwise. Going counter-clockwise would require pushing the like poles even closer together first, which the spring force of attraction won't allow. The rotor always turns clockwise.

Key principle: The offset rest position is built into the shape of the stator. It is a mechanical solution to a directional problem ‚Äî no electronics required.
5.2
Why Pulses ‚Äî Not Continuous Current

Once the rotor has been spun to its new position, the stator's permanent magnetism is enough to hold it there ‚Äî even with no current flowing. The rotor is magnetically "locked" in place until the next pulse arrives.

This means the coil only needs current for a very brief moment ‚Äî just a few milliseconds ‚Äî to flip the rotor. Then the current is switched off until the next second arrives.

Benefit of short pulses
Most of the time, zero current flows through the coil. The circuit draws only its small background current. This is why a tiny battery can power a watch for over a year.
Cost of long pulses
If the pulse is longer than necessary, the coil draws current needlessly. Battery life shortens directly in proportion. Manufacturers design pulses to be as short as physically possible.
Chapter 6 ¬∑ The Crystal

The Quartz Oscillator

The heart of all accuracy. A tiny piece of quartz shaped like a tuning fork, vibrating 32,768 times per second ‚Äî consistently, reliably, for years.

6.1
Why 32,768 Hz?

This specific frequency is chosen because 32,768 = 2¬π‚Åµ ‚Äî it is exactly 2 multiplied by itself 15 times. This matters because dividing a frequency by 2 electronically is extremely simple. Divide 32,768 by 2 fifteen times, and you get exactly 1 ‚Äî one pulse per second.

... ‚Üí 64 ‚Üí 32 ‚Üí 16 ‚Üí 8 ‚Üí 4 ‚Üí 2 ‚Üí 1 Hz
15 stages of √∑2. Each stage is just a few transistors. Elegantly simple.
6.2
Why Quartz is Ideal for Timekeeping
Natural oscillator
Like a tuning fork, quartz vibrates at a precise natural frequency when disturbed. The quartz crystal's shape and size determine this frequency ‚Äî and it doesn't change easily.
Elasticity restores it
A pendulum swings back because of gravity. Quartz vibrates back because of its own elastic properties ‚Äî the crystal material itself acts as the restoring spring.
Temperature stable
Metal expands and contracts significantly with temperature, changing a balance spring's stiffness. Quartz is far less affected ‚Äî its frequency barely drifts with temperature.
Position doesn't matter
Because the restoring force is crystal elasticity (not gravity), the vibration frequency is the same regardless of orientation. A mechanical watch rate changes in different positions.
Chapter 7 ¬∑ The Crystal

Quartz has a remarkable property: bending it creates electricity, and passing electricity through it makes it bend. This two-way relationship is what allows the circuit to both detect and sustain the crystal's vibration.

7.1

Bend the quartz ‚Üí voltage appears.
As the crystal vibrates and bends, it generates a small alternating voltage. The faster it vibrates, the higher the frequency of this voltage. The circuit uses this to detect and track the vibration.

Apply voltage ‚Üí quartz bends.
The circuit uses this direction to give the crystal a tiny "push" each cycle, sustaining its vibration. Without these regular pushes, the vibration would eventually die out ‚Äî like a pendulum without its escapement.

Chapter 8 ¬∑ The Battery

Cells & Capacitors

Every quartz watch needs a power source. Three options exist ‚Äî primary cells, secondary (rechargeable) cells, and capacitors. Each has distinct characteristics.

8.1
The Three Types
Primary Cell
Use once, discard. Chemical reactions inside produce electricity. Once all the reactive material is used up, it's flat ‚Äî the reactions can't be reversed. Standard watch batteries are primary cells.
Secondary Cell
Rechargeable. Uses reversible chemical reactions. When flat, forcing current backwards through it restores the electrode materials. Can be charged and discharged hundreds of times. Used in solar and kinetic watches.
Capacitor
Stores charge, not chemistry. Charges and discharges rapidly. Stores much less energy than a cell of similar size, but has one key advantage: its voltage drops proportionally as it discharges ‚Äî so you can read the remaining charge by measuring voltage.
Seiko Kinetic
Generates electricity from wrist movement, stores in a capacitor or rechargeable cell. Seiko later developed special rechargeable cells whose voltage falls as they discharge ‚Äî giving charge indication without needing a capacitor.
Why does the capacitor's falling voltage matter? Standard cells maintain near-constant voltage until almost completely flat ‚Äî you can't tell how much charge remains until the watch stops. A capacitor's voltage falls steadily, so the watch can display a real remaining-charge indicator. More complex electronics, but more information for the wearer.

Chapter 10 ¬∑ The Battery

EOL Indication & Disposal

A good watch gives you warning before it stops. End-of-life indication is a simple but clever design feature built into nearly all modern quartz watches.

10.1
End-of-Life (EOL) ‚Äî How It Works

Most quartz cells maintain an almost constant voltage right up until they are nearly flat ‚Äî then the voltage drops steeply. Because the voltage stays constant for so long, you can't predict how much life remains by simply measuring it.

But the steep drop at the end can be detected. The circuit monitors battery voltage and when it falls below a threshold (typically around 1.3 V), it activates the EOL indication.

Analog watch EOL
The seconds hand starts fast-stepping ‚Äî jumping 2, 3, 4, or 5 seconds at a time, then pausing. The watch still keeps correct time, but the unusual motion tells you a new battery is needed.
Digital watch EOL
A low battery symbol appears on the LCD display, often flashing. Some watches also reduce their display functions to conserve the remaining power.
EOL indication is a useful service tool: When a watch comes in with an unusual seconds hand stepping pattern, check EOL first before diagnosing any mechanical or electronic fault. Replace the battery ‚Äî the symptom may disappear entirely.
10.2
Responsible Battery Disposal

Watch batteries contain materials that are harmful to the environment ‚Äî including silver oxide, which reacts with soil and groundwater. They should never be thrown in regular waste. Options:

1
Collect and sell in quantity
If you service many watches, accumulate batteries and sell them to a registered scrap battery dealer. They contain recoverable silver with real market value.
2
Local Authority Recycling Centre
For small quantities, take to your local recycling centre and deposit in the designated battery collection bin. This is the minimum acceptable option.
3
Retailer take-back schemes
Many electronics and watch retailers accept used batteries for recycling. Check with local shops ‚Äî many are legally required to take them back.

Treat it as a black box. The integrated circuit either works or it doesn't. It cannot be serviced or repaired ‚Äî only replaced. On most movements, the circuit module lifts off and a replacement drops in. Always handle circuits by their edges ‚Äî static discharge and mechanical shock can kill them.
11.2
Why Some Watches Don't Step Every Second

Quartz analog watches without a seconds hand ‚Äî many ladies' watches ‚Äî often step the stepper motor once every 10, 15, 20, or even 30 seconds instead of once per second.

This dramatically reduces battery drain because the motor coil (the biggest current consumer) is pulsed far less often. The gear train ratio is adjusted to compensate ‚Äî the motor still drives the minute and hour hands correctly despite the slower stepping.

Benefit
Far lower battery consumption. A smaller battery can be used, enabling a smaller, slimmer movement ‚Äî important for dress and ladies' watch designs.
Tradeoff
No seconds hand is possible ‚Äî the hand would jump in large leaps. Only suitable for watches displaying hours and minutes only.
11.3
The Inhibit (Stop) Switch

When you pull the crown out to the time-setting position, a small switch activates inside the movement. This switch connects a terminal on the circuit board to the main plate, which signals the integrated circuit to stop sending pulses to the stepper motor.

The train stops immediately. You can then wait for an exact moment and push the crown back in ‚Äî the watch starts precisely on cue. This allows time setting to within one second of accuracy.

Chapter 12 ¬∑ The Circuit

Rate Setting & Inhibition

Quartz crystals aren't perfectly accurate ‚Äî they can be slightly fast or slow. Inhibition is the elegant solution that tunes the rate without any mechanical adjustments.

12.1
The Problem ‚Äî Crystal Tolerance

A quartz crystal specified as 32,768 Hz might actually vibrate at 32,767.3 Hz to 32,768.7 Hz ‚Äî a tolerance of ¬±20 parts per million (ppm). That tiny variation translates to nearly 2 seconds per day of error ‚Äî far more than most users would accept.

12.2
The Old Solution ‚Äî Trimmers

Older quartz watches used a small adjustable component (a trimmer capacitor) on the circuit board to fine-tune the crystal frequency. Rotating the trimmer slightly changed the crystal's effective frequency. Useful, but: it added cost, complexity, and a potential failure point.

12.3
The Modern Solution ‚Äî Inhibition

Modern watches use a smarter approach:

1
Crystal runs deliberately fast
The crystal is manufactured to vibrate slightly above 32,768 Hz ‚Äî even at the low end of its tolerance range, it runs fast. This ensures the watch always gains time without inhibition.
2
Circuit drops occasional pulses
The integrated circuit is programmed to "inhibit" (skip) one or more pulses from the crystal at precise regular intervals. Skipping a pulse means the motor doesn't step ‚Äî the watch pauses for a fraction of a second.
3
Rate calibrated at manufacture
During production, each movement's actual crystal frequency is measured. The number of pulses to skip is set in the circuit to tune the net rate precisely. High-quality inhibition movements can achieve accuracy within 1 second per week.
‚ö†
Inhibition causes an odd timing machine reading. The watch runs slightly fast for a few seconds, then pauses briefly ‚Äî then fast again. This is completely normal and correct. Always check the manufacturer's recommended measurement interval before concluding there is a rate problem. A standard measurement interval may give a misleading fast or slow reading.

The Quartz Gear Train

The quartz gear train looks similar to a mechanical watch train ‚Äî but it works in a fundamentally different way. Understanding the differences matters for servicing.

13.1

13.2
Key Differences from a Mechanical Train
Driven from the fast end
In a mechanical watch, the mainspring drives from the slow end (barrel). In a quartz watch, the stepper motor drives from the fast end (rotor). This means almost no torque develops anywhere in the train.
Plastic is fine
Without torque, there's no significant wear. Plastic wheels, pinions, and even pivots are used extensively ‚Äî they're cheap to injection-mould and many plastics are self-lubricating. Jewel count is often zero.
No jewels needed
Jewels prevent wear in mechanical watches where torque is high. With almost no torque and often self-lubricating plastic components, jewels are unnecessary. Jewel count is no longer a quality indicator in quartz watches.
Vulnerable to jamming
Because the motor produces very little power, almost no reserve torque exists. A speck of dust or dried lubricant can jam the train completely. Good case sealing is important ‚Äî dirt ingress is a common service cause.
Diagnostic tip: A quartz watch that stops intermittently or whose battery life is shorter than expected often has a partially jammed train. Measure the current consumption ‚Äî if it's higher than specified, the motor is working harder to overcome friction. Service the train.
Chapter 14 ¬∑ The Train

Motion Work & Keyless Work

Setting the hands and date on a quartz watch uses familiar components ‚Äî but with some important differences from a mechanical watch, particularly in how the cannon pinion is driven.

14.1
The Cannon Pinion Difference

In a mechanical watch, the cannon pinion is friction-fit onto the centre arbor and the arbor turns continuously to drive the hands. In a quartz watch, the centre arbor doesn't exist in the same way ‚Äî a driving wheel is turned by the third pinion in the train.

The cannon pinion is fitted between the two "arms" of the driving wheel. While the watch is running, the whole assembly turns together. When setting the hands, the driving wheel is held stationary by the stopped train ‚Äî and the cannon pinion is turned against the friction grip, slipping through the arms. Same friction principle, different mechanical arrangement.

14.2
The Three Crown Positions
Pos 1
Stem fully pushed in ‚Äî winding position
In a quartz watch, turning the crown in this position has no effect ‚Äî the watch is battery powered and needs no winding. Some movements engage a crown-operated function (e.g., alarm set) in this position, but the basic function is neutral.
Pos 2
Stem pulled out one click ‚Äî date correction
The setting mechanism engages a "three-toothed date corrector" via the date setting wheel. Turning the crown (usually anticlockwise) advances the date indicator directly. Useful for rapid date correction without running through all 24 hours.
Pos 3
Stem pulled out fully ‚Äî time setting
The sliding pinion engages the setting wheel on the dial side. This drives the minute wheel and cannon pinion directly. The inhibit switch also activates in this position ‚Äî stopping the motor so you can set time precisely and restart at the exact moment.
The stop lever: When the stem is pulled to position 3, a stop lever engages a train wheel to physically arrest the train, while the switch contact signals the circuit to halt pulsing. Both mechanical braking and electronic stopping happen simultaneously.
Chapter 15 ¬∑ The Train

The Date Change Mechanism

The date changes automatically at midnight via a chain of wheels driven from the hour wheel. Understanding this mechanism helps diagnose date-related faults.

The date change mechanism is located under the dial. The hour wheel is the starting point ‚Äî it completes one rotation every 12 hours. Through an intermediate date wheel and a pinion, it drives the date indicator driving wheel, which takes 24 hours to complete one rotation.

1
Hour wheel rotates
The hour wheel (1 rotation per 12 hours) drives the intermediate date wheel, which drives the date indicator driving wheel (1 rotation per 24 hours).
2
Driving wheel engages the date ring
The date indicator driving wheel has a projection that engages one tooth on the date indicator ring once per 24-hour rotation. This advances the date ring by exactly one day.
3
Date jumper completes and locks the change
The date jumper serves three purposes: it helps snap the date indicator into the next position when it's close, it holds the date indicator completely still for the other 23+ hours, and it ensures the date number is centred precisely in the window.
Common fault ‚Äî date changes at wrong time: The date should change at midnight. If it changes at noon instead, the hour wheel is 12 hours out of phase ‚Äî set the time to 12:00 AM rather than PM, let it run through midnight to reset the date, then set the correct time.
Chapter 16 ¬∑ Display

Liquid Crystal Displays

No backlight, no power to show an image ‚Äî LCDs work by blocking light rather than producing it. Extremely low power, extremely versatile, and surprisingly simple to troubleshoot.

16.1
How an LCD Actually Works

An LCD is a sandwich: two thin glass sheets with a liquid crystal layer between them. Transparent electrodes on the inner surfaces create the segments of each digit. In their normal state, these segments are transparent ‚Äî light passes through and reflects off a silver backing, making the segment appear as a bright background colour.

When a voltage is applied to a segment, the liquid crystals in that area align differently, making the segment opaque. Light can no longer pass through ‚Äî the segment appears dark (black). This is how characters are formed: select which segments to make opaque, and you get the desired digit or symbol.

Important: LCDs don't generate light ‚Äî they modulate it. This is why they're invisible in the dark without a backlight, but also why they use almost no power in normal use. The backlight uses far more power than the display itself.
16.2
The Conductive Rubber Strip ‚Äî Most Common Fault

The LCD electrodes connect to the circuit board via a special conductive rubber strip ‚Äî a zebra-striped component with alternating conductive and insulating layers. It carries electricity vertically (from circuit to LCD) but not horizontally.

This strip must make clean contact at both surfaces. Dirt, dust, oxidation, or simple misalignment causes one or more segments to fail ‚Äî or the entire display to go blank.

1
Clean circuit board terminals with a dry leather buff stick
The terminals may have a thin oxide film. Rub gently 2‚Äì3 times with a dry buff stick. Never use abrasives ‚Äî these terminals are delicate.
2
Clean the rubber strip surfaces
Use a blower to remove dust. If needed, a soft leather buff or cloth. Never use cleaning fluids on the rubber strip ‚Äî solvents cause it to swell permanently, ruining the component.
3
Clean LCD terminal edge
Same gentle dry approach. Ensure no fibres or dust remain on any surface before reassembly ‚Äî these can break the electrical contact.
4
Reassemble and test
In most cases, cleaning restores a faulty digital display completely. If individual segments remain permanently opaque (black patches) after cleaning, the LCD itself is damaged ‚Äî replace it.
